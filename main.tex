\section{Introduction}

Consider a sequence of requests $R_1, R_2, \dots, R_T \in \Requests$. For each
request we have to make one of $N$ decisions. We make a simple assumption that
the requests are sampled i.i.d. from a fixed, but unknown, distribution $D$.
Each request is an $N \times M$ matrix with entries in the interval $[0,1]$. In
other words, we assume that the space of requests is $\Requests = [0,1]^{N
\times M}$. We would like to come up with a (randomized) policy $\pi$ that
maximizes a linear reward subject to linear constraints. Formally, a randomized
policy $\pi$ is a function $\pi:\Requests \to \Delta_N$, where
$$
\Delta_N = \left\{ x \in \R^N ~:~ x_1, x_2, \dots, x_N \ge 0, \sum_{i=1}^N x_i = 1  \right\} \: .
$$
We would like to come up with a policy that is an (approximate) optimal solution
of the following optimization problem.
\begin{align*}
\maximize_{\pi} \qquad & \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \sum_{i=1}^N R_{t,i,1} \cdot \pi(R_t)_i \\
\subjectto \qquad & \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \sum_{i=1}^N R_{t,i,j} \cdot \pi(R_t)_i \le B_j & \text{for all $j=2,3,\dots,M$} \\
\end{align*}

The value $R_{t,i,1}$ is the reward collected for $t$-th request if we make
decision $i$ for it. The value $R_{t,i,j}$ for $j \ge 2$ is the $j$-th business
metric for the $t$-th request. The right-hand sides of the constraints $B_2,
B_3, \dots, B_M$ are given to us head of time.

The optimization is over the space of all possible policies. However,
for technical reasons that will become obvious later, we consider only
policies that are measurable.

For example, at Uber, a request can be a request by a customer to get a ride and
Uber needs to choose one of $K$ possible prices for the request. $R_{t,i,1}$
could be the probability of the trip happening, $R_{t,i,2}$ could be the price
paid by the rider, $R_{t,i,3}$ could be the price paid to the driver, etc. The
right-hand sides of the constraints would be business objectives Uber wishes to
achieve over a longer time period, say, one day or one week.

The optimization problem can be expressed as a standard linear program:

\begin{align*}
\maximize_{x_{1,1}, \dots, x_{T,N} \in \R} \qquad & \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \sum_{i=1}^N R_{t,i,1} \cdot x_{t,i} \\
\subjectto \qquad & \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \sum_{i=1}^N R_{t,i,j} \cdot x_{t,i} \le B_j & \text{for all $j=2,3,\dots,M$} \\
& \sum_{i=1}^N x_{t,i} = 1 & \text{for all $t=1,2,\dots,T$} \\
& x_{t,i} \ge 0 & \text{for all $i=1,2,\dots,N$ and all $t=1,2,\dots,T$}
\end{align*}

However, as opposed to a standard linear program, we don't have all the
coefficients $R_{t,i,j}$ available upfront. Instead, the requests come one at a
time, and for each request we need to choose the probability vector $(x_{t,1},
x_{t,2}, \dots, x_{t,N}) \in \Delta_N$. In order to make the decision for $t$-th
request quickly, the probability vector is computed on the fly using the policy
$\pi$ applied to the request $R_t$.

We focus on a regime where the number of requests $T$ is much greater than both
$N$ and $M$. The assumption that the entries of a request matrix $R_t$ lie in
$[0,1]$ translates to an assumption that each request has negligible impact on
the total (or average) business metrics. For the applications that we are
interested in, the i.i.d. assumption is a good approximation for short enough
time periods (e.g. a day or a week). Perhaps the most problematic assumption is
that the value of the reward and business metrics $R_{t,i,j}$ are known at the
time when the decision $\pi(R_t)$ needs to be made. In applications we have in
mind, the reward and the business metrics are predicted by machine learning
models at time of the decision, and the actual reward and other business metrics
are revealed only after the decision has been made, sometimes with a significant
delay. However, in order to keep our model simple, we assume that $R_t$ is
available at the time when the decision for $t$-th request needs to be made, and
we assume that the entries in $R_t$ are perfectly accurate.

We also make the simplifying assumption that the number of requests $T$ is known
upfront. In applications, this value would be predicted by a machine learning
model.

For technical reasons, we assume that distribution $D$ is non-atomic, i.e., does
not assign positive probability to any single point in $\Requests$. The reason
for this assumption will become clear later. This technical assumption can be
lifted at the expense of much more elaborate proofs.

We use the following notation for the rest of the paper. For $i=1,2,\dots,N$, we
define $e_i$ to be the vector in $\R^N$ with $i$-th entry equal to $1$ and all
other entries equal to zero, that is,
$$
e_i = \underbrace{(0,\dots,0,1,0,\dots,0)}_{\text{$1$ is at position $i$}} \: .
$$

\section{Lagrangian policies}

We consider policies parametrized by $M-1$ non-negative real numbers $\lambda_2,
\lambda_3, \dots, \lambda_M$. As will become clear later, these are the dual
variables associated with the constraints of the linear program. However, for
now, think of them as arbitrary non-negative real numbers. For any $(M-1)$-tuple
$\lambda = (\lambda_2, \lambda_3, \dots, \lambda_M)$ we define a policy
$\pi_\lambda:\Requests \to \Delta_N$ as follows. For any request $R \in [0,1]^{N
\times M}$, we define
$$
\pi_\lambda(R) = e_i
\qquad \text{where} \qquad
i = \argmax_{i=1,2,\dots,N} R_{i,1} + \lambda_2 R_{i,2} + \lambda_3 R_{i,3} + \dots + \lambda_M R_{i,M} \: .
$$
We call a policy of this form a \emph{deterministic Lagrangian policy}, as it
corresponds to a Lagrangian relaxation of the linear program. We defined the
\emph{deterministic Lagrangian policy space} $\Pi_\Lambda^\text{det}$ as the set
of all possible policies of this form. Formally, that is,
$$
\Pi_\Lambda^\text{det} = \left\{\pi_\lambda ~:~ \lambda \in \R^{M-1}, \lambda \ge 0, \lambda = (\lambda_2, \lambda_3, \dots, \lambda_M) \right\} \: .
$$

Note that policies in $\Pi_\Lambda^\text{det}$ are deterministic and measurable.

\section{Linear programs}

We consider the following linear program and analyze its properties.

\begin{align*}
\maximize_{x_{1,1}, \dots, x_{T,N} \in \R} \qquad & \sum_{t=1}^T \sum_{i=1}^N a_{t,i,1} \cdot x_{t,i} \\
\subjectto \qquad & \sum_{t=1}^T \sum_{i=1}^N a_{t,i,j} \cdot x_{t,i} \le B_j & \text{for all $j=2,3,\dots,M$} \\
& \sum_{i=1}^N x_{t,i} = 1 & \text{for all $t=1,2,\dots,T$} \\
& x_{t,i} \ge 0 & \text{for all $i=1,2,\dots,N$ and all $t=1,2,\dots,T$}
\end{align*}

The dual of this linear program is the following linear program:

\begin{align*}
\minimize_{\substack{\mu_1, \dots, \mu_T \in \R \\ \lambda_2, \dots, \lambda_M \in \R}} \qquad & \sum_{t=1}^T \mu_t + \sum_{j=2}^M B_j \lambda_j \\
\subjectto \qquad
& \mu_t + \sum_{j=2}^M a_{t,i,j} \lambda_j  \ge a_{t,i,1} & \text{for all $i=1,2,\dots,N$ and all $t=1,2,\dots,T$} \\
& \lambda_j \ge 0 & \text{for all $j=2,3,\dots,M$}
\end{align*}

\section{Optimization problem for the expected values}

Recall the $D$ is a distribution over requests. We assume that a request $R$ is
sampled from $D$ at random. We consider the following optimization problem over all possible
measurable policies $\pi:\Requests \to \Delta_N$.

\begin{align*}
\maximize_{\pi} \qquad & \Exp_{R \sim D} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^N \pi(R)_i \cdot R_{i,1} \right] \\
\subjectto \qquad & \Exp_{R \sim D} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^N \pi(R)_i \cdot R_{i,j} \right] \le B_j & \text{for all $j=2,3,\dots,M$} \\
\end{align*}

\begin{lemma}
For any measure $D$ with finite support, there exists a (possibly randomized) Lagrangian policy $\pi$ that is optimal.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}

number of requests $R_1, R_2, \dots, R_T$ with positive probability.
